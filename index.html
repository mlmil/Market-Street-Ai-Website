Philosophy

Agent Versus Chat

A chat is when you ask a question and move on with your day. An agent is when you delegate actual work. Here is why that distinction matters for your business.

Read full issue

When is something an agent, and when is something just a chat?

Here is the simplest way I can put it:

A chat is when you ask a question and move on with your day. An agent is when you delegate actual work.

That is the whole headline. But if you want to understand why this distinction matters - and why every single one of my clients is suddenly asking about it - let me use an analogy you already understand.

The Drive-Thru vs. The Kitchen

Think of a chat like the drive-thru window at a fast food joint. You pull up, you say what you want, somebody hands it to you, and you drive away. Quick. Transactional. Does the job.

An agent is more like hiring a line cook and giving them the keys to your kitchen. You do not stand over their shoulder. You do not hand them each ingredient one at a time. You say "make the specials for today" and walk away. When you come back, there is a menu ready and the dishwasher is running.

One is a question. The other is a delegation.

Both are useful. They are just not the same thing, and most people are only using one of them.

Three Things Changed Recently

Three forces converged recently that made agents impossible to ignore.

1. A better UI. For a long time, if you wanted to use an AI agent, you had to talk to it through a terminal. But Claude released Claude Cowork, and ChatGPT followed with Codex. Both look like the chat apps you already use. Finally - no terminal required.

2. Better AI models. Claude Opus 4.6 and OpenAI GPT 5.4 are both dramatically better at running long, complex tasks without screwing things up. Agents need stamina. Now they can go the distance.

3. People switched tools, and stumbled into agents. A wave of users moved from ChatGPT to Claude, discovered Claude Cowork, and that has been accelerating the whole trend.

You Already Have the Tools

If you have a Claude subscription, you already have access to Claude Cowork and Claude Code. If you have a ChatGPT subscription, you already have access to Codex. You just need to download the desktop app.

Yes, it has "code" in the name. No, you do not need to be a coder.

The Four Unlocks

Unlock 1: Files. In chat, you are the bottleneck - uploading files, copying text, hitting limits. With agents, you put the AI in a folder full of 200 files and it finds what is relevant. You are not the mule anymore.

Unlock 2: Memory that compounds. Chat memory is shallow and static. Agents accumulate context across sessions - how you work, what your clients need, what mistakes to avoid. It goes from a tool to an asset.

Unlock 3: Read and write to real systems. Chat can look. Agents can touch - your calendar, your CRM, your Gmail. Record a meeting, drop in the transcript, and the agent drafts action items, updates the CRM, and puts a follow-up email in your drafts.

Unlock 4: Skills - stacked, not solo. Spin up sub-agents running in parallel: one does intake analysis, another runs competitive research, a third checks brand guidelines. They finish faster and each is sharper.

When to Stay in Chat vs. Switch to Agents

Stay in chat for conversational iteration, one-off tasks, things that fit in the context window, and team collaboration.

Switch to agents when you have more than 10 files, need read AND write to your systems, the task is repetitive, or you want memory that compounds.

Homework

1. Download the desktop app. 2. Create a sandbox folder. 3. Give the AI a task in plain English and sit back.

Bottom line: A chat is a question you ask and walk away from. An agent is work you hand off and trust to get done. You already have both. The question is whether you are using the second one yet.

Strategy

The Power of the Little Fires

How small AI victories build big business success. The secret to leveraging AI is not a single monumental breakthrough - it is putting one fire out at a time.

Read full issue

In the exciting, sometimes overwhelming world of Artificial Intelligence, it is easy to feel the pressure to implement massive, game-changing AI solutions overnight. But what if the secret to truly leveraging AI in your business is not a single, monumental breakthrough, but something far more achievable?

It is time to embrace the philosophy of "putting one fire out at a time."

Why Little Victories Are the Key to AI Adoption

Think about managing a large fire. Trying to put out an entire raging blaze all at once is overwhelming. A better strategy is to tackle one small, manageable source of heat at a time.

The same principle applies to integrating AI into your business. Trying to overhaul every department with a single, complex AI system is a recipe for frustration.

1. Start with a Single Pain Point

Do not try to automate your entire customer service department tomorrow. Identify one specific bottleneck causing you the most immediate frustration. Example: Instead of "We need an AI marketing strategy," start with: "We need an AI to draft 5 variations of our weekly social media captions."

2. Prove the Value, Gain Trust

Once you have achieved that first small win, you have proven the concept works. Use the successful small project to gather data. Track how much time was saved, how much faster content was produced. These measurable results become your strongest arguments when you ask for the next, slightly bigger investment.

3. Iterate and Scale

With a proven, successful small AI tool in place, scaling becomes much less intimidating. If you successfully implemented AI for content drafting (Fire 1), you can move to Fire 2: analyzing customer feedback. Then Fire 3: automated follow-up emails. You build an integrated system piece by piece.

Your Action Plan

This week, ask yourself: What is the single most tedious, repetitive task in my business right now? What is the smallest piece of AI I could use to handle just that one task? What is the measurable result of that first small win?

By focusing on these small victories - putting one fire out at a time - you will not just deploy AI; you will build a resilient, successful, and sustainable AI-powered future for your business.

Judgment

The Greatest AI Skill Nobody Talks About: Knowing When Not to Use It

Everyone is rushing to tell you what AI can do. Very few people are telling you when to put it down. The real advantage is knowing which problems to hand to the AI, and which ones to keep for yourself.

Read full issue

Everyone is rushing to tell you what AI can do. Very few people are telling you when to put it down.

That is the skill that actually matters. Not the prompt engineering. Not the latest model. Not the flashiest automation. The real advantage - the one that separates people who get value from AI and people who waste their time - is knowing which problems to hand to the AI, and which ones to keep for yourself or your team.

The Prep Cook, Not the Chef

Think of AI like a prep cook in a restaurant kitchen. A good prep cook chops the onions, portions the sauces, lays out the mise en place, and gets everything organized before the dinner rush. That work has to happen, it is tedious, and it does not require the head chef judgment.

But you do not hand the prep cook the final plate. You do not have them walk out to the dining room and sweet-talk the VIP customer. You do not let them decide whether the salmon is done.

AI is your prep cook. It handles the repetitive, tedious work that has to get done but does not need your judgment. You - or your employees - handle the plating, the customer conversation, and the call on whether something is good enough to ship.

Do Not Automate the Wrong Things

A friend of mine works at a commercial real estate company in Grand Rapids, Michigan. Twenty buildings, maybe sixty employees. He started messing with Claude on the side, just to see what it could do.

One of his coworkers was drowning in tedious work. Every month she had to read through commercial leases, extract specific clauses, and transfer that information from her department to another. It was necessary work. It was also a pain in the ass.

He did not build a fancy automation. He sat with her, helped her write out her workflow as a simple instruction file, and tested whether Claude could follow it. It could. He estimates it saved her a sixth of her time every year. Roughly $10,000 in labor - gone.

But he did not fully automate it. He left human checkpoints at every layer. The AI does the grunt work, a person reviews the output, and only then does it move forward. Sometimes the smartest automation is no automation at all.

When to Think, When to Offload

When should you offload your thinking to AI, and when is it more efficient to do the thinking yourself?

You cannot just hand everything over. If you build a fully automated pipeline for a complex task and the AI does not have enough context at step seven, it will confidently produce garbage. And you will not catch it because you stopped looking.

The framework is simple: automate the parts where you know what a good output looks like, and keep your hands on the parts where judgment is required.

Put Yourself Out of a Job (On Purpose)

Every day, try to automate a piece of your own job. The expense reports, the file sorting, the first-draft emails, the data extraction. Get the AI to handle it.

You free up time, which you fill with new work - work that is harder to automate, because it requires judgment, relationships, or creative decisions you cannot template. The stuff you cannot automate is the stuff that is actually valuable.

You essentially turn their product roadmap into your development team.

The Human Checkpoint Is the Point

Most people think the goal is to remove humans from the process entirely. No. That is the trap.

The AI gets you 90% of the way there in seconds. The last 10% is where you earn your money. The review step is what keeps you learning. Every time you catch a mistake, you learn something about what the AI does not understand yet.

Amplify people. Do not replace them. The AI is the prep cook. The human is still the expediter.

The One-Sentence Summary

The greatest AI skill is not knowing every tool. It is knowing your own problems well enough to say: this part, the AI handles. That part, a person handles. And I know exactly where the line is.
