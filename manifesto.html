Manifesto

AI Should Work for Everyone.

AI is moving fast. The tools are extraordinary. And if we're not paying attention, the people who benefit from them and the people who get left behind by them are going to be sorted by nothing more than who could afford the subscription. That's not a technology problem. It's a values problem. And values are something we can choose to get right.

These are mine.

What I Stand For
01

Inclusive Governance — Not Closed-Door Control

The decisions being made right now about how AI works, who it works for, and who gets to shape it are being made in rooms most people will never enter. That has to change. Governments, workers, independent creators, and the communities most affected by these tools all deserve a seat at the table — not as an afterthought, but from the beginning.

02

A Just Transition for Workers

AI is going to change work. It already is. But the cost of that transition shouldn't fall on the people who can least afford it. Reskilling, labor protections, and economic policies that prioritize people over displacement — these aren't optional extras. They're the whole point. If the productivity gains from AI don't reach workers, we've failed.

03

AI Literacy for Everyone — and I Mean Everyone

I spend about $400 a month on AI tools right now. There is no version of my younger self that could have afforded that. Someone without a job, without reliable internet, without a personal device — they're doing this on a library computer with a free-tier account that barely scratches the surface. That gap is growing. If we don't address it deliberately, AI literacy becomes another thing that sorts people into haves and have-nots. The tools in everything I build are free or low-cost, and the skills transfer regardless of platform — because AI literacy should be a foundation, not a luxury.

04

AI That Strengthens Democracy

AI should make governments more responsive, more transparent, and more accountable to the people they serve — not more efficient at ignoring them. Public services built on AI need to be auditable, explainable, and subject to human judgment. Technology that makes it easier to govern people without listening to them is not progress.

05

Sustainable AI for a Livable Planet

The compute required to run these systems has a real environmental cost. That cost needs to be on the balance sheet, not externalized onto the rest of us. Energy-efficient AI, reduced carbon footprints, and using these tools to help address climate challenges rather than accelerate them — that's not idealism. It's engineering responsibility.

06

AI That Makes Us More Human — Not Less

Here's what I keep coming back to: there's a real risk that in making the case for how powerful AI is, how much you need it, how lost you'd be without it — we start to sound like every other piece of technology designed to capture your attention and never let go. I don't want that.

I play guitar. I read books. I walk on grass, pet dogs, go to movies, tinker with things, sit with kids. The analog world isn't something to escape from. It's the whole reason any of this matters. What I actually believe — and what I've seen in practice — is that AI used right has the potential to pull us back from the techno-stress world that phones and social networks created. Not pull us deeper in. Get the busywork out of the way so you can close the laptop and go be a person. Reduce screen time in the name of actual productivity so you can spend more time in real contact with real people. That's the version of AI worth building toward.

07

This Is Who We're Talking About

The AI conversation happens mostly in cities, mostly in offices, mostly among people who already have options. This manifesto is about everyone else.

Farm workers whose labor feeds a country that rarely says their names. Immigrants who built industries they were never invited to lead. Women who have outworked, outperformed, and been underpaid at every table they were allowed to sit at. Communities of color who have watched every wave of economic progress crest just out of reach. Workers without degrees who have decades of skill that no algorithm has figured out how to measure. People who can't afford to get sick, let alone afford the healthcare system that's supposed to help them when they do.

These are not edge cases. These are the majority of the workforce. And they are the last people anyone in the AI industry thinks about when they're building the next thing.

AI has the potential to change that — to be a tool that doesn't care what you look like, where you're from, or what school you went to. A tool that levels the field instead of tilting it further. But that doesn't happen by accident. It happens because people decide it matters and do the work to make it real.

Civil rights aren't a legacy issue. Healthcare isn't a privilege. Equal pay isn't a negotiation. And access to the tools that define the modern economy isn't something anyone should have to fight for.

08

Safety, Alignment, and Human Agency

As these systems get more powerful, safety and alignment with human values aren't negotiable features — they're the floor. AI should extend what humans can do, not quietly replace human judgment in the places where human judgment is exactly what's needed. The human stays in the loop. Always.

Work-Ready AI exists because I believe everyone deserves a seat at the table in the AI economy — not someday, not in theory, but right now, with tools that work on a library computer and skills that belong to the person who learned them permanently.

This is the work. Let's do it.
